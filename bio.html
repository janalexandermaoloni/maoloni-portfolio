I'm Alexander Maoloni, a web & media designer and front-end developer. For years I've been drawing interfaces and shipping the code behind them, under the name Hexsole. This page is the long version: how I got here, what I actually do, and where I learned it.

How it started

I came to the web the way a lot of designers do: I wanted the things I imagined to actually exist, not just live in a mockup. So I taught myself to code. What began as curiosity, pushing pixels in the browser at 2am, turned into a proper craft, and eventually into a one-person studio.

Today I work with founders, small teams and studios who need a website, a product interface or a full brand identity built quickly, without the usual back-and-forth between “the designer” and “the developer.” Here, they're the same person.

Design and code, one head

Most of my work lives at the seam between design and engineering. I sketch the layout, choose the type and colour, draw the illustrations, then write clean, hand-authored HTML, CSS and JavaScript to make it real, fast and responsive on every screen.

No hand-offs, no “that's not how I designed it,” no bloated page builders. The result is tighter, loads faster, and keeps a single, coherent voice from the first sketch to the live site.

What I do best

I focus on three things: websites (marketing sites, landing pages, portfolios), software (product UI and front-end for web apps) and branding (logos, identities and design systems). They overlap constantly, and that's the point, a brand that ships as a real, working site.

Along the way I care a lot about the details most people never name: type rhythm, motion that earns its place, accessibility, and performance. Good work should feel effortless precisely because a lot of effort went into it.

Education & formation

My background is in web & media design, a formal diploma programme covering web design, front-end development, visual communication and digital media. It gave me the grammar; real projects gave me the fluency.

I'm a compulsive self-teacher on top of that: type, motion, accessibility, new front-end tooling. The web changes every year, and staying sharp is part of the job description.
